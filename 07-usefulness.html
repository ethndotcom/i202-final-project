Chapter 07

The Usefulness of a Sociotechnical Approach

Mutual shaping · Two solutions

§ 7.1How Sociotechnical Thinking Helped

Looking at algorithmic hiring through the mutual shaping lens Dr. Jake introduced in weeks 3 and 4 — using the Elaine Herzberg case to illustrate why technological determinism fails — changes what this project is actually about (Course slides, Weeks 3–4). Without the lens, this is a story about "AI hiring bias" — a technical problem with technical fixes.

With the lens, the problem becomes the entire sociotechnical system: the applicant behaviors the ATS has shaped, the recruiter incentives that make algorithmic filtering attractive, the vendor business models that profit from efficiency rather than candidate outcomes, the legal and regulatory vacuum that allows proprietary black boxes to make employment decisions, and the historical hiring patterns baked into the training data.

Mutual shaping specifically helped me see that applicants are not passive victims of the algorithm; we adapt to it. We write resumes for robots. We memorize keywords. We practice video interviews to perform the affect the camera wants. The technology shapes us, and our adaptations in turn shape how the technology evolves.

That is a very different story than "the AI is biased," and it points to very different solutions.

§ 7.2Proposed Solutions

Solution 01

Mandatory transparency & the right to explanation

Every applicant rejected by an automated employment decision tool should receive, at minimum: (a) notification that an automated tool was used, (b) the top factors that contributed to the decision, and (c) a pathway to request human review. NYC Local Law 144 and the EU AI Act gesture in this direction but stop short of a full right to explanation. A federal standard would begin to rebalance the power asymmetry by opening the black box — at least partially — to the people it is being used on.

This is a sociotechnical solution rather than a purely technical one because it changes both the technology (new logging and reporting requirements) and the social infrastructure (new legal rights, new HR practices, new candidate expectations). It doesn't "fix" the algorithm; it changes who has agency inside the system, redistributing some of the power that currently sits exclusively with employers and vendors.

Solution 02

A human-in-the-loop floor for rejection decisions

Algorithms should be allowed to rank and triage, but a human should make the final rejection decision for any candidate who meets a minimum qualification threshold. Full algorithmic rejection with no human review should be prohibited for employment decisions that involve protected classes.

This is directly analogous to the 2024 ACLU settlement in the Robert Williams case from the week 9 Project Green Light material, in which the Detroit Police Department was prohibited from arresting people based solely on facial recognition results (Course slides, Week 9). Both cases recognize that some decisions are too consequential to leave to an algorithm alone.

This solution acknowledges the efficiency case (algorithms can sort 50,000 resumes; humans cannot) while refusing the harm case (algorithms should not silently reject candidates before any human ever sees them). It also re-introduces the social layer that was missing from my fifty-plus algorithmic rejections: the sense that someone on the other end actually considered me. It is the same layer that was present at CFS, and the reason that process produced a different outcome.

Fifty-plus algorithmic pipelines produced zero interviews. One human pipeline produced an internship. The difference is the entire system.
