Themed module · Stakeholders · Power · History

§ 1.1Themed Module & Rationale

For this final project, I am analyzing the sociotechnical system of algorithmic hiring, with a specific focus on the use of Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS), automated resume screening, and one-way video interviews in the internship application pipeline for college students. I chose this module for two reasons. First, in the week 7 slides introducing algorithmic decision-making, Dr. Jake listed "AI systems used to determine who to interview and hire" as one of the three algorithmic sociotechnical contexts the course would examine, alongside the Los Angeles homeless-housing system and Detroit's Project Green Light (Course slides, Week 7, slide 43).

Second, algorithmic hiring is the sociotechnical system I have personally collided with the most over the past year. As a sophomore Informatics major at Indiana University applying to technology consulting and IT consulting internship roles across automotive, healthcare, banking, data, and other industries, I submitted more than fifty applications. Nearly every single one was processed first by an ATS, and some required a one-way video interview (the one used by Northwestern Medicine is the one I remember most clearly). I was rejected or ghosted by every company whose pipeline involved algorithmic screening.

One company rejected me within an hour of submitting my application — a response time no human recruiter could achieve. The only company whose process was entirely run by humans was the only one that ever gave me a first-round interview, and I am now completing an internship with them (Creative Financial Staffing). That contrast is the empirical foundation for this entire analysis.

This project uses sociotechnical thinking, which Dr. Jake defined in the opening week of the course as "a set of analytical tools and perspectives in problem solving to carefully consider the relationship between people and technologies in order to create more nuanced, well rounded and well-developed solutions to real world problems" (Course slides, Week 2, slide 5). The goal is to treat algorithmic hiring not as a purely technical system but as a sociotechnical one, a term Dr. Jake defined as "an adjective that describes something as simultaneously social and technical" (Course slides, Week 2, slide 4).

Algorithmic hiring surfaces nearly every concept from the I202 sociotechnical toolkit: stakeholders and social actors, agency, power, the sociotechnical flower (digital artifacts, material artifacts, social norms, social practices, laws and policies), culture and technoculture, infrastructure, disciplinary and persuasive architecture, models, algorithms, the black box, classification systems and categories, social analytics (race, gender, class, disability), mutual shaping, and a rejection of both technological determinism and reductionist thinking.

§ 1.2Stakeholders & Social Actors

Dr. Jake defined a stakeholder as "a person or a group of people who have something to lose or to gain in a particular situation; or who have an interest or concern regarding a particular situation," and he specified that not-alive objects are not stakeholders (Course slides, Week 2, slide 14). He defined social actors separately as "those who do things, cause things, or have agency," noting that social actors are usually (but not always) human (Course slides, Week 3, slide 11). Agency itself he defined as "the capacity to take actions and make choices" (Course slides, Week 3, slide 13).

The algorithmic hiring system has many stakeholders, of which a smaller set function as empowered social actors:

Applicants
Students, early-career workers, and experienced professionals submitting resumes and completing video interviews. Stakeholders with very limited agency.
Hiring Companies
The automotive, healthcare, banking, and data companies I applied to. They purchase the ATS software, configure the filters, and own the final hire/no-hire decision. Stakeholders and powerful social actors.
ATS & AI Vendors
Private companies like Workday, Greenhouse, iCIMS, and HireVue. They build and sell the resume-parsing algorithms, keyword filters, and video interview analysis tools. Both stakeholders and powerful social actors, because they define what the system is capable of at the model level.
Recruiters / HR
Humans who in theory review candidates after algorithmic filtering. In high-volume pipelines, most never see an applicant the algorithm has not pre-approved.
Hiring Managers
Team leads making final decisions from the shortlist the algorithm produces.
Regulators
The EEOC, New York City's Local Law 144, Illinois's AI Video Interview Act, and federal agencies trying to govern algorithmic bias in employment.
Career Services
University advisors coaching students on how to get past ATS filters, shaping student resume-writing practices in response to the technology.
CFS
Creative Financial Staffing — the staffing firm I now intern with. CFS represents a contrasting model within the same system: a hiring pipeline that foregrounded human judgment over algorithmic filtering.

What stakeholders are trying to accomplish

  • Applicants want a fair shot at a job, a clear signal about fit, and ideally feedback when rejected.
  • Hiring companies want to fill roles quickly and cheaply. They are measured on time-to-fill and cost-per-hire.
  • Vendors want to sell software and grow market share. Their success metric is efficiency gains for the employer, not candidate outcomes.
  • Recruiters want to hit hiring quotas without being overwhelmed by application volume.
  • Regulators want to ensure hiring practices do not discriminate against protected classes under Title VII and the ADA.
  • University career services want their students to get jobs, so they teach ATS-optimization as a practical skill.

§ 1.3Power & Agency

Dr. Jake defined power as "the ability to influence," noting that "influence leads to consequence, which social informatics is interested in understanding from a nuanced point of view" (Course slides, Week 6, slide 23). Power and agency are closely related, and, importantly, technologies themselves can have power even though social informatics generally ascribes agency specifically to human beings (Course slides, Week 6, slide 23).

In algorithmic hiring, power is radically asymmetric. — A working thesis

Hiring companies and vendors hold the overwhelming majority of both power and agency. Vendors have the power to define what "qualified" even means computationally — which keywords matter, which facial expressions score higher, which linguistic patterns correlate with "success." Employers have the power to set criteria, the agency to change the process at any time, and the power to reject without explanation. The technology itself exerts power: a resume parser that cannot read your PDF has consequences for you regardless of any human's intent.

Applicants, by contrast, have almost no power and only a narrow sliver of agency. I can choose whether to apply, and I can try to tune my resume for ATS keywords, but I have no power over the criteria, no visibility into the scoring, and no ability to appeal a rejection.

§ 1.4History of the System

General history

Applicant Tracking Systems began in the 1990s as digital filing cabinets for HR departments. As online job boards made it trivially easy to apply to dozens of jobs at once, application volumes exploded and HR teams could no longer read every resume. The ATS evolved from passive storage into active filter, first using Boolean keyword matching and later using machine learning to rank and reject candidates automatically.

One-way (asynchronous) video interview platforms emerged in the early 2010s. HireVue, one of the largest vendors, marketed AI that could analyze a candidate's facial expressions, tone of voice, and word choice to predict job performance. After widespread criticism and a 2019 complaint filed by the Electronic Privacy Information Center, HireVue announced in early 2021 that it would stop using facial analysis, though it continues to use audio and linguistic analysis (Harwell).

Amazon's 2018 scrapped AI hiring tool is the best-known cautionary tale. Amazon trained a model on a decade of hiring data and discovered the model had taught itself to downgrade resumes containing the word "women's" because the historical training data reflected a male-dominated workforce (Dastin). Amazon scrapped the tool, but only after years of use. The model was doing exactly what it was trained to do; the training data was the discriminatory artifact.

My specific case

Over the past year I applied to more than fifty roles — technology consultant and IT consultant / manager internship positions — across automotive manufacturing, healthcare systems, banks, data companies, and other industries. Every application went through an online portal backed by an ATS. A subset required a one-way video interview; Northwestern Medicine's is the one I remember most clearly because I recorded myself answering questions with no human on the other end. I was rejected or ghosted by every company that used AI-driven screening.
