Chapter 04

How Power Works in the System

Same system, six experiences

The same algorithmic pipeline produces six entirely different experiences depending on where you stand inside it.

Dr. Jake emphasized across weeks 6 and 7 that power and agency are closely related but distinct, and that the same system produces very different experiences for people holding different amounts of each (Course slides, Week 6, slide 23; Week 7, slide 10). Algorithmic hiring makes this easy to observe. What follows is the same system, viewed from six positions.

Vendor engineer at HireVue or Workday

You have near-total power over the rules. You decide what counts as a "good" candidate at the model level. You don't see the human cost of false rejections because you measure success in hiring-manager satisfaction, not rejected-applicant experience.

Hiring manager at a Fortune 500 company

You have real power but limited agency over the algorithm. You benefit from not having to read 5,000 resumes. You trust the shortlist. You rarely ask how candidates got filtered out, and you have no tool to audit the filter.

Recruiter

You have some agency to override the algorithm but are pressured by volume to trust it. You're measured on time-to-fill and cost-per-hire. You are not measured on how many good candidates got filtered out before you ever saw them.

Well-resourced applicant

The ATS still processes you, but you have shortcuts. You can get an employee referral and skip the ATS entirely. You can afford career coaches. You have alumni networks, professors who know hiring managers, and family connections that provide side doors. The ATS is disciplinary architecture for people who have to walk through the front door.

Under-resourced applicant

The ATS is the front door, and it is locked. You have no referral, no career coach, no alumni mentor. You apply to 100 jobs, get 100 automated rejections, and have no way to know whether your resume is the problem, the algorithm is the problem, or you were filtered out at random.

My own experience

I sit somewhere in the middle. I attend a well-regarded university (IU's Luddy School), I have real work experience (Global Link IT), I hold multiple virtual consulting certifications (BCG, PwC, Accenture, Deloitte, Datacom), and I have a professional portfolio. None of that mattered to the ATS at the companies I applied to.
