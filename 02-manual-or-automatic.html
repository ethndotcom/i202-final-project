Manual or Automatic?
Disciplinary or Persuasive?

Models · Algorithms · Architecture

§ 2.1What Is Manual vs. What Is Automated

In the week 7 lectures on algorithms and architectures, Dr. Jake defined a model as "an abstract representation of some process" (citing O'Neil; Course slides, Week 7, slide 54) and an algorithm as "a computerized analysis that makes use of a mathematical representation of a process (model) to analyze data and suggest an outcome" (Course slides, Week 7, slide 46). Algorithmic hiring is exactly this: models of "the successful candidate" are built from historical data, and algorithms use those models to score, rank, and filter live applications.

Automated

  • Resume parsing — extracting name, education, experience, and skills from the uploaded file.
  • Keyword matching and weighting against the job description.
  • Initial ranking or filtering of candidates (reject, hold, or advance).
  • Disqualification based on knockout questions (work authorization, GPA thresholds, years of experience).
  • In video interviews: transcription of responses, linguistic analysis, scoring against a trained model, and (formerly) facial expression analysis.
  • Sending the automated rejection email — or, more often, sending nothing at all and leaving the applicant in silence.

Manual (in theory, if you clear the filter)

  • Recruiter review of the algorithmically-approved shortlist.
  • Phone screens and live interviews.
  • Final hire decisions.
The sociotechnical point — for the majority of applicants, the hiring process is entirely automated. No human ever sees the resume. The one-hour rejection I received is the clearest possible proof: no human read those materials and decided I was not qualified inside of sixty minutes. In my experience, the "manual" stage never began — zero out of more than fifty ATS-screened applications advanced to a human.

§ 2.2Disciplinary & Persuasive Architecture

Dr. Jake defined disciplinary architecture as "an object or system that, through its design, directly regulates or controls behavior or human action," using the example of a brick wall that cannot be run through or a locked door that cannot be pushed open (Course slides, Week 7, slide 33). He defined persuasive architecture, by contrast, as something "designed to provoke, suggest, or discourage actions for people to take" — designs that sway behavior but do not force it (Course slides, Week 7, slide 34). Algorithmic hiring uses both.

Disciplinary architecture

The ATS itself is disciplinary architecture. If your resume does not contain the right keywords, or if it fails to parse correctly (bad formatting, images, tables, unusual fonts), you are removed from the pipeline with no option to appeal, explain, or try again for that role. The system simply refuses, exactly the way the brick wall in Dr. Jake's example refuses to be run through.

Similarly, the one-way video interview is disciplinary in format: you cannot ask clarifying questions, you cannot negotiate the time limit, and you cannot opt out without withdrawing your application entirely.

Persuasive architecture

The ecosystem surrounding the ATS is persuasive. LinkedIn's "skills" feature nudges you to list keywords that match employer search queries. University career centers and resume-writing guides encourage students to mirror the job description's language. "ATS-friendly resume" templates circulate online, steering applicants toward specific formats. None of these things force anyone to do anything, but they shape behavior in the direction the system prefers.

Students learn to write resumes for robots, not humans.

This is mutual shaping in action — the theoretical lens Dr. Jake introduced in week 3: the technology shapes the social practice of applying for jobs, and the social practice in turn shapes how the technology evolves (Course slides, Week 3).

§ 2.3Algorithms & the Shape of Action

Dr. Jake emphasized in week 7 that algorithms, just like physical architecture and infrastructure, both allow for and limit human action (Course slides, Week 6, slide 2; Week 7, slide 48). The ATS illustrates this directly. It affords employers the ability to screen thousands of applications in seconds. That same ATS affords applicants almost nothing: no transparency, no feedback, no appeal. The one-way video interview affords the employer standardized data — every candidate answers the same question in the same time — but strips from the candidate the things a normal interview allows for: rapport, clarification, the ability to read the room.

The same object allows one group to do much more while simultaneously limiting what the other group can do.
