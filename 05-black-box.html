Chapter 05

Parts of the System
That Have Been Black Boxed

Inputs · Outputs · Access

Dr. Jake defined a black box in the week 8 lectures as "a process where the individual steps of the process are invisible to various actors within a sociotechnical system. Data are inputted and then undergo some sort of analysis or process and come out altered. The analysis or process is unclear to users and to subjects of the process" (Course slides, Week 8, slide 9). Algorithmic hiring is saturated with black boxes.

§ 5.1What Processes Are Obscured

Resume scoring
The ATS assigns each resume a fit score against the job description. Applicants never see this score, never see which keywords mattered, and cannot request the number.
Knockout questions
Some applications silently reject candidates for answers to questions like "Are you willing to relocate?" or "What is your expected salary?" Candidates are not told those questions were knockout criteria.
Video analysis
When I recorded my Northwestern Medicine video interview, I had no idea what was being scored. Word choice? Facial expressions? Pauses? Eye contact? Length of response? The vendor does not disclose this, and the employer often does not know the details.
Ranking & sorting
Even candidates who pass the initial filter are ranked against each other. The ranking algorithm is proprietary.
The decision itself
Often there is no explicit rejection. You simply never hear back. The one-hour rejection is a rare case where the black box produced a fast, unambiguous signal. Most of the time, the black box's output is silence.

§ 5.3Who Can See Inside

Have access

  • Vendor engineers (HireVue, Workday, etc.) who built the models.
  • Employer administrators who configure the filters, though they rarely understand the underlying machine learning.
  • Auditors working under laws like NYC Local Law 144, which requires bias audits of automated employment decision tools.

Do not have access

  • Applicants. We are the subject of the decision and have no view into it.
  • Most recruiters and hiring managers, who see the output shortlist but not the scoring logic.
  • Regulators in most U.S. jurisdictions. Federal law has not caught up with AI-driven hiring.
  • University career services. They coach students based on best guesses about how the ATS works, not insider knowledge.
The asymmetry is the point. The people most affected by the decision have the least ability to see how it was made.

This parallels the analysis of Project Green Light from the week 9 case study, in which the residents of Detroit most affected by facial recognition surveillance were the stakeholders with the least ability to inspect the system (Course slides, Week 9, slides 42–43).
