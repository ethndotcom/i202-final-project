Chapter 06

Desired & Undesired Outcomes

Classification · Social analytics · Harm

§ 6.1What the Designers Wanted

The people who built and bought these systems had real problems to solve. The desired outcomes are reduce time-to-fill, reduce cost per hire, manage overwhelming application volume (a single posting at a major employer can receive tens of thousands of applications), provide consistent and "standardized" evaluation, and — marketed but rarely delivered — reduce bias in hiring by removing subjective human judgment.

The efficiency case is real. No human team can read 50,000 resumes. Some form of filtering is inevitable at scale.

The question is not whether filtering happens but who designs it, who controls it, and who gets to see it.

§ 6.2Classification, Categories, Social Analytics

In the week 8 lectures on social analytics and classificatory systems, Dr. Jake defined an analytic as "a lens through which we can make sense of observations" (Course slides, Week 8, slide 30), and classification systems as "sets of boxes (metaphorical or literal) into which things can be put into then do some kind of work — bureaucratic or knowledge production" (citing Bowker and Star; Course slides, Week 8, slide 35). A category, in his framing, is "a group of people or things with particular features in common" (Course slides, Week 8, slide 36). He emphasized that our learned classification systems build our mental models, which in turn shape the computational models used to train predictive algorithms (Course slides, Week 8).

That sequence — mental model → computational model → predictive algorithm — is the story of algorithmic hiring in one sentence.

Algorithmic hiring is a classification system in exactly this sense. It sorts applicants into "qualified" and "not qualified," categories that are mutually exclusive (per Dr. Jake's criteria) and that carry enormous embedded assumptions. Dr. Jake's week 8 social analytics — race, gender, class, and disability — are directly applicable.

Gender

The Amazon case is the clearest demonstration (Dastin). Trained on a decade of male-dominated tech hiring, the model learned that women were worse candidates and began penalizing the word "women's" on resumes. The classification "qualified software engineer" had been shaped historically by gender, and the algorithm absorbed that pattern. Even after Amazon scrapped the specific tool, resume parsers still interact with gender through name inference and women-coded extracurriculars — what Dr. Jake's week 8 lecture on the technologies of names describes as names doing "invisible work" that communicates gender (Course slides, Week 8, slide 23).

Race

Studies have found that resume-screening algorithms can pick up on race through name proxies, zip codes, and school names — including historically Black colleges and universities versus predominantly white institutions (Wilson and Caliskan). The system does not need to know your race explicitly to discriminate on the basis of it. Again, this is the technologies-of-names dynamic: names are not neutral data, they carry social signals that the algorithm is happy to read.

Class

ATS systems routinely filter on school prestige, through explicit GPA or school-name filters or through ML models trained on hires from a small set of target schools. Per Dr. Jake's definition, class is "a classification system that groups people with similar life chances, shared historical narratives, living circumstances, and/or who occupy a similar position in the economic system" (Course slides, Week 8, slide 48).

A filter on school prestige is a class filter dressed up as a merit filter.

Disability

Dr. Jake defined disability as "a classification system that groups people with similar body conditions," distinguishing a medical model from other approaches (Course slides, Week 8, slide 49). One-way video interviews penalize candidates with speech differences, facial differences, neurodivergence, or non-standard affect. A candidate on the autism spectrum who doesn't make typical eye contact, or a candidate with a stutter, is disadvantaged by a model trained on neurotypical "success" candidates.

§ 6.3Culture, Technoculture, & Signs

Dr. Jake opened week 5 with Anne Balsamo's definition of culture as "a socially shared symbolic system of signs and meanings" (Balsamo, p. 5; Course slides, Week 5, slide 5), and introduced technoculture as the idea that "technology and culture shouldn't be thought about as in opposition to each other, but rather they are interlinked" (Course slides, Week 5, slide 17).

This is a useful frame for the resume. A resume is not a neutral document — it is a carefully arranged set of cultural signs, and the ATS is a machine that reads signs it was trained on. If your signs don't match the training set (wrong school, wrong extracurricular vocabulary, wrong format), you read to the machine as illegible. The applicant and the ATS are both inside a technoculture: neither exists independently of the other, and each reshapes the other over time.

§ 6.4Harms to Stakeholders

  • Candidates are rejected silently, with no feedback, no recourse, and no way to improve, because they don't know what they did "wrong."
  • Protected classes experience automated discrimination at scale, with the scale itself making individual cases hard to detect.
  • Qualified candidates are filtered out for cosmetic reasons (resume formatting, missing keyword), and companies never know they missed them.
  • Candidates internalize rejection as personal failure when the rejection may be algorithmic noise.
  • Trust in the hiring process erodes, producing application fatigue and disengagement from the labor market.
I spent months assuming something was wrong with me. The moment I hit a human-run pipeline (CFS), I got an interview and now an internship. The problem was not me.

§ 6.5Resisting Determinism & Reductionism

Dr. Jake defined technological determinism in the opening weeks of the course as the belief that "technological development follows a natural logic, which is beyond cultural or political influence" and that "these developments impact societies and force them to adapt" (Course slides, Week 3, slide 16). He separately defined reductionist thinking as "oversimplification of human beings and social phenomena to easily quantified data points that makes computational modeling possible" (Course slides, Week 2, slide 28).

Algorithmic hiring is often described reductively — "the algorithm is biased, fix the algorithm" — or deterministically — "AI is just the future of hiring, applicants need to adapt." Both framings are wrong.

The algorithm is one node in a much larger system that includes applicant behavior, recruiter incentives, vendor business models, legal voids, and historical hiring patterns baked into training data. And applicants are not simply being forced to adapt to an inevitable technology — their adaptations, in turn, are shaping how the technology evolves. That is mutual shaping, and it is the lens Dr. Jake told us social informatics requires (Course slides, Week 3).
