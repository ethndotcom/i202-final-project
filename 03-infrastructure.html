Chapter 03

The Infrastructure of the System

Artifacts · Norms · Practices · Law

§ 3.1Social & Technical Infrastructure

Dr. Jake defined infrastructure in week 6 as "elements of a built environment that make human society possible," describing it as "a system of substrates, often invisible until made visible via problems, workarounds, or other forms of human activity" (Course slides, Week 6, slide 1). Algorithmic hiring runs on both technical and social infrastructure, and its invisibility is one of the reasons it is so hard to contest.

Digital artifacts
Cloud-hosted ATS platforms (Workday, Greenhouse, iCIMS, Lever, Taleo) running on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud. Resume-parsing libraries and natural language processing models. Machine learning models trained on historical hiring data. Video recording and streaming infrastructure for asynchronous interviews. Speech-to-text and linguistic analysis APIs. Candidate profile databases, often retained indefinitely and shared across employers using the same vendor. Integration layers between ATS, LinkedIn, Indeed, and corporate career sites.
Material artifacts
The servers in data centers that run the ATS and video interview platforms. The applicant's own laptop, webcam, microphone, and the home or dorm environment they are required to record in. The physical office space of the company where the successful candidate will eventually work — which the algorithm has decided who does and does not enter.
Social norms
Widespread acceptance in HR that high-volume roles require algorithmic screening. The normalization of ghosting — applicants no longer expect a rejection letter. The disappearance of the thank-you note as a professional expectation.
Social practices
Student resume-tailoring for the algorithm rather than the recruiter. Applying to fifty or one hundred jobs as a standard practice. Performing a particular affect on camera because a model is scoring it.
Laws & policies
Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, prohibiting discrimination in employment. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), which interacts awkwardly with one-way video interviews that penalize atypical affect or speech. NYC Local Law 144 (2021), which requires bias audits of automated employment decision tools. Illinois's Artificial Intelligence Video Interview Act. The EU's AI Act, which designates employment algorithms as high-risk systems.

§ 3.2The Sociotechnical Flower

The sociotechnical flower is the I202-specific tool Dr. Jake gave us in week 3 to decompose a sociotechnical problem into its stakeholder-centered components (Course slides, Week 3). Applied to algorithmic hiring, the flower organizes as follows: stakeholders at the center, with petals for each kind of artifact, practice, norm, and policy. No petal can be removed without changing what the technology does in practice. That is the sociotechnical lens in one image.

§ 3.3Examples of Infrastructure Breakdown

Infrastructure, according to Dr. Jake's definition, becomes visible through problems, workarounds, and breakdowns (Course slides, Week 6, slide 1). Algorithmic hiring provides several.

Northwestern Medicine, the silent return

The clearest breakdown in my experience is the Northwestern Medicine one-way video interview. I completed the interview, hit submit, and never heard back. No rejection, no advance, no acknowledgment. From my side, the system looked like it was working: I recorded my video, it uploaded, I got a confirmation screen. But the social infrastructure that is supposed to surround a hiring decision — a recruiter, a feedback loop, a human being on the other end — had been replaced by a one-way data pipe.

The breakdown is not that the technology failed; the breakdown is that the technology worked exactly as designed while the social contract around hiring quietly disappeared.

The one-hour rejection

The one-hour rejection from a different employer is a second kind of breakdown. The technical infrastructure did what it was built to do — parsed my resume, scored it, and issued a rejection. What broke was the social promise of consideration. A rejection that arrives within an hour is a rejection that was never weighed by a person.

Amazon, at scale

On a larger scale, the Amazon scrapped hiring tool is a famous infrastructure breakdown (Dastin). The technical system did what it was trained to do. What broke was the assumption that the training data — a decade of hiring decisions in a male-dominated field — was a neutral description of "qualified." The historical social infrastructure of bias got hard-coded into the technical infrastructure and scaled.
